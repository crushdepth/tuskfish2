Biofloc systems: a field primer

14 June 2026 | by L. Tran | Marine, Systems

Biofloc culture works by feeding the bacteria, not just the fish. By holding a high carbon-to-nitrogen ratio in the water, the system encourages dense microbial communities that lock up waste ammonia and turn it into protein-rich floc the animals graze on between feeds. Done well, it slashes water exchange and trims the feed bill. Done carelessly, it crashes.

Starting a system

Most failures happen in the first three weeks, before the microbial community has stabilised. Seed the water, dose carbon conservatively, and resist the urge to stock heavily until the floc volume holds steady across a full day.

Daily checks are non-negotiable: dissolved oxygen, settled floc volume, and a quick visual on colour and smell. A system that turns green is drifting toward algae; a system that smells sour has gone anaerobic in the corners. Both are recoverable if you catch them on the same day.

When it goes wrong

The hardest lesson is that biofloc punishes inconsistency more than it punishes mistakes. A single missed carbon dose rarely matters; a pattern of them will swing the system until it can't buffer the load. Keep a log, keep aeration redundant, and never let the water sit still.
